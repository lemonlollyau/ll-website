How to rank for 'near me' searches in Adelaide

By the lemonlolly team·20 July 2026·5 min read

"Near me" is the phrase every business owner wants to rank for. Someone types "physio near me" into their phone, and whoever comes up gets the call. It feels like the most valuable search there is — and it more or less is.

So the instinct is to put "near me" on the website. Sprinkle it through the homepage, work it into the page titles, maybe build a page called Physio Near Me Adelaide.

That does nothing. Worse than nothing, really, because it makes your copy read like it was written by a machine having a bad day.

"Near me" isn't a keyword

Here's the bit that changes how you think about it. Google doesn't look for the phrase "near me" on your website, because "near me" isn't describing your business. It's describing the searcher.

When someone types it, Google treats it as an instruction: use where this person is right now. It then finds businesses that are relevant, close, and prominent enough to be worth showing. Your page never needed to contain those two words at any point.

Which is quite freeing, once it lands. There's no phrase to chase. There's just the ordinary work of being a well-described, well-regarded business in a known location.

You don't optimise for "near me". You optimise for being the obvious answer when someone nearby asks.

What actually decides who shows up

Three things, and only one of them is under your full control.

  • Where the searcher is. Not your call. Someone in Mawson Lakes gets Mawson Lakes options. This is the single biggest factor and no amount of clever marketing shifts it.
  • Whether Google understands what you do. Entirely your call. Mostly your Google Business Profile categories and your website's plain description of your services.
  • Whether Google thinks you're any good. Mostly reviews — how many, how recent, how detailed — plus mentions and links from elsewhere.

So the honest strategy for "near me" searches is: nail the two things you control, and accept that geography does the rest.

Start with the profile, because it does the heavy lifting

Almost every "near me" search returns a map with three businesses at the top. Those come from Google Business Profiles, not from websites. If your profile is thin, wrongly categorised or unverified, your website's quality is close to irrelevant for these searches.

The primary category is doing more work than anything else on that profile. It's what makes you eligible for "physio near me" rather than "massage near me". Get it wrong and you're invisible for the search you care about while ranking nicely for one you don't.

Work through the 12-point checklist if you haven't recently. And if you're already set up but simply can't find yourself, there's usually a specific reason — a duplicate listing, a lapsed verification, a quiet suspension.

Name real places instead

Since "near me" is a dead end as a phrase, the thing to actually write on your website is the set of places you serve. Real suburb names, in normal sentences, on pages where they make sense.

"We look after homes across Prospect, Nailsworth and Sefton Park" is a sentence a human can read and a sentence Google can use. It's specific, it's true, and it does the job that stuffing "near me" was supposed to do.

If you cover a wide area, dedicated suburb pages can extend your reach further — but only if each one has something genuinely local to say. Copy-pasting the same page with the suburb swapped is the classic doorway-page mistake, and Google has filtered that pattern for years.

Reviews are the tiebreaker

When several businesses are all close enough and all relevant, reviews decide the order. This is the part people underestimate.

What matters isn't just the star average. It's volume, recency, and whether the reviews mention what you actually do. A review saying "fixed our hot water on a Sunday in Woodville" is worth more than five saying "great service" — it contains the service and the place, in a customer's own words.

You don't get those by asking for "a review". You get them by asking a question: would you mind mentioning what we did and where? More on asking without being a pest.

Then make sure the phone rings

"Near me" searches are overwhelmingly mobile, and mobile searchers are impatient. They're standing in a driveway or sitting in a car. If your site takes five seconds to load, they're already back on the results page tapping the next result — a slow page costs you the customer before Google is even involved.

So: tappable phone number, visible without scrolling. Suburbs you serve, obvious. Hours, current. That's most of it.

How to check without fooling yourself

Searching from your own desk tells you almost nothing — Google knows it's you, and you're standing at your own address.

Instead, ask a few customers or friends in different suburbs what they see when they search your service plus "near me". That's real data, gathered in about ten text messages. Then watch the calls and direction requests in your profile insights over the following months. Those numbers are much closer to money than any ranking report.

One last honest note: if you're in a suburb with four competitors on the same street, you will not always be top of the map, and no one can promise you otherwise. What you can do is be the best-described, best-reviewed option in the area you actually serve — which, most of the time, is enough.
