How to rank for 'near me' searches in Adelaide

By the lemonlolly team·9 July 2026·5 min read

"Coffee near me." "Plumber near me." "Physio near me." Type any of those into a phone in Adelaide and Google hands back a neat little list — a map, three businesses, some star ratings. If yours isn't on it, you're invisible at the exact moment someone is ready to buy. The good news: "near me" isn't a mysterious code you have to crack. It's mostly a handful of signals you can start influencing this week.

Here's what "near me" actually keys off, and how a small business can win it.

"Near me" isn't really about the words

Here's the thing most people get wrong: you don't rank for "near me" by putting "near me" all over your website. Google stopped needing those exact words a long time ago. It quietly adds the searcher's location to nearly every local query. Someone searching "hairdresser" while standing in Prospect gets Prospect-flavoured results whether or not they typed "near me". So ranking for "near me" really means ranking for local intent — being the obvious nearby answer when someone close by is looking.

Google works that out using three ingredients: relevance (do you match what they searched?), distance (how close are you to them?), and prominence (how well-known and trusted do you seem?). You can't move your shopfront, but you have real control over the other two.

Start with your Google Business Profile

Your Google Business Profile is the single biggest factor in whether you turn up in the map results at all. For "near me" searches especially, it does the heavy lifting. Claim it, verify it, and fill in everything — hours, phone, website, description, opening date, and above all your primary category. That category tells Google what you do and carries enormous weight. If you're a mobile business or you cover a patch of suburbs, set your service areas properly rather than leaving it to guesswork. We've written a full walkthrough on why your Adelaide business isn't showing up on Google Maps if the basics need a tidy-up first.

Make your location unmistakable

Distance is measured from the searcher to you, so Google has to be certain where "you" is. Give it no room for doubt:

  • Put your full address in the footer of every page, in plain text (not baked into an image).
  • Name your actual suburb on your homepage and contact page — "our studio in Norwood", not just "servicing greater Adelaide".
  • If you serve multiple areas, say which ones, honestly. A cleaner covering the eastern suburbs can list Burnside, Kensington and Magill — but only areas you genuinely work in.
  • Keep your name, address and phone number identical everywhere they appear online. Mismatches quietly erode trust.

Reviews are the "near me" tiebreaker

When two businesses are a similar distance away and both look relevant, reviews often decide who lands in that top-three map pack. A steady trickle of recent, genuine Google reviews signals that you're active, real and trusted. Ask every happy customer — in person, in a follow-up text, on the receipt — and reply to each one. Recency and consistency matter more than a giant total, so a few fresh reviews a month beats a big pile from three years ago.

The business that wins "near me" isn't always the closest. It's the closest one Google trusts.

Write for how locals actually search

On-page content still matters for the blue links below the map. You don't want robotic "plumber near me Adelaide near me" copy — you want natural language that matches how people search: your service plus your area. Think "emergency electrician in the eastern suburbs" or "wedding photographer, Adelaide Hills". Create a genuinely useful page for each core service, mention the suburbs you serve where it reads naturally, and answer the questions customers actually ask.

Quick win

Grab your phone, turn on location, and search your main service the way a customer would — "<your service> near me". Screenshot who shows up in the top three. Those are your real local competitors, and their profiles will show you exactly what "complete and active" looks like in your category.

What about "open now"?

A chunk of "near me" searches carry urgency — people wanting something right now. Google leans on your listed hours to decide who's currently open, and it will happily bump an open business above a closed one. So keep your hours accurate, including public holidays and the odd Fringe-season late night. Nothing kills a first impression faster than a customer driving over to a "near me" result that turns out to be shut.

Give it time — and keep showing up

Some changes move fast. Fix a wrong category or complete your profile and you can see a shift within days. Building real prominence through reviews and consistency is slower — usually weeks to a few months. Local search rewards the businesses that keep turning up, not the ones chasing a one-off hack.
