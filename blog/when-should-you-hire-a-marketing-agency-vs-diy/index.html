When should you hire a marketing agency vs DIY?

By the lemonlolly team·21 July 2026·5 min read

We're a marketing agency, so you already know how this article is supposed to end. Every business needs an agency, ideally this one, ideally on a retainer, starting Monday.

The real answer is that timing matters. Bring someone in at the right moment and the money compounds. Bring them in too early and you pay to polish something that isn't finished yet. Getting that timing right is worth more than any single campaign.

So here's the honest version of the decision.

When DIY still makes sense

There's a stage where doing it yourself is genuinely the better investment — usually the first year or two.

  • You're still working out what you sell. If your offer, your pricing or your ideal customer is still moving, marketing it is premature. You'll pay someone to polish a message you're about to change.
  • You're the product. Consultants, coaches, trades with a strong personal following — your face and your voice are doing the work. Outsourcing that tends to flatten it.
  • The basics aren't done yet. If your Google Business Profile is half-filled and you've got eleven reviews, fixing those beats any clever campaign. It's free, it takes an afternoon, and it makes everything you do afterwards work harder.

That last one matters more than people expect. A surprising share of local marketing results come from work you can do yourself in a weekend — the whole picture is in our local SEO guide, and the highest-leverage hour is making sure you actually appear on Google Maps.

Worth being clear about one thing though: "not yet" has a shelf life. The businesses that stay in DIY mode longest are usually the ones who never quite get around to it, and a year of good intentions costs more than a few months of help.

If a business can't name what it does differently in one sentence, an agency can't fix that with advertising. That's a business question, not a marketing one.

The signs it's time to bring someone in

The switch usually isn't about ambition. It's about one of these becoming true:

  1. Marketing is now costing you more in hours than in dollars. If you're editing reels at 10pm after a nine-hour day, you're paying for marketing already — just in the currency you can least spare.
  2. You've plateaued and can't see why. Enquiries flat for six months, nothing obviously broken. Diagnosing that is a skill, and it's hard to do from inside your own business.
  3. You're spending real money on ads without knowing what's working. Once you're past a couple of thousand a month, the cost of guessing exceeds the cost of expertise.
  4. You need something you can't make. A website that actually converts, a brand that doesn't look like a template, a proper campaign. These are craft jobs.
  5. Consistency has collapsed. You post brilliantly for three weeks whenever it's quiet, then vanish for two months when work picks up. That rhythm undoes itself, and it's the single most common reason small-business marketing fails.

Number four is worth pausing on. Sometimes what looks like a marketing problem is a website problem — plenty of traffic, no enquiries. Before hiring anyone for campaigns, it's worth checking whether your site is converting the visits you already have. Fixing that is usually cheaper than buying more traffic.

The middle option nobody mentions

It isn't a binary. The arrangement that works best for most Adelaide businesses under about twenty staff is a split.

You keep the things that need to be you: showing up on socials, replying to comments and messages, asking happy customers for reviews, knowing your regulars by name. Nobody outsources that well, and the attempts read as hollow.

You hand over the things that need tools, time or specialist skill: the website, the brand, paid ads, anything technical. These are the jobs where an amateur hour costs more than a professional one.

Reviews are the clearest example of something to keep in-house. It costs nothing, it moves your local ranking, and it works far better coming from the person who did the job than from a third party — here's how to ask without being a pest.

These are ballparks from our own market, not survey data — treat them as a smell test, not a benchmark. If a quote sits far outside them in either direction, ask why. Suspiciously cheap usually means offshore templating; very expensive should come with a very clear explanation.

Questions worth asking yourself first

Before you contact anyone, three honest answers will save you money:

  • Can I fund this for six months? Marketing compounds. Three months in and out is close to setting the money on fire.
  • What does success look like, in numbers? "More leads" isn't a brief. "Twelve qualified enquiries a month" is.
  • Am I prepared to be involved? The best agency work happens when the client answers questions, supplies photos and gives real feedback. Hand it over entirely and it comes back generic.

If you can answer all three, you're ready — and the sooner you start, the sooner the compounding does its work. If you can't answer them yet, spend a month getting clear on them rather than a year. Writing regularly is one of the highest-return things you can do in the meantime, and it still works in 2026.

And if you read the second list and recognised three of them, you probably already knew the answer before you opened this.

Agency or DIY, answered

Two tests. First, can you afford to spend consistently for at least six months? Marketing that stops and starts wastes most of what you put in. Second, is marketing now costing you more in time than it would in fees? If you're doing it at 10pm and doing it badly, that's the signal.

Not usually, until you need more than about three days of marketing work a week. One person also can't be a designer, copywriter, ads specialist and developer at once, so you tend to get one of those skills well and the rest approximately. In-house makes sense when the volume is steady and the work is narrow.

Yes, and it's often the smartest option. Keep the things that need your voice and your presence — posting, replying, asking for reviews — and outsource the things that need tools or specialist skill, like your website, ads or brand. Any decent agency will tell you which is which.
