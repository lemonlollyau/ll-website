How to set up Google Search Console (and what to check weekly)

By the lemonlolly team·24 July 2026·5 min read

Most small businesses fly completely blind on search. They have a website, they vaguely hope Google likes it, and they've no idea what people actually type before they land on it — or whether they land on it at all.

Google gives you a free tool that answers exactly that, and hardly anyone outside the industry uses it. It's called Search Console, and setting it up is one of those fifteen-minute jobs that quietly pays off for years. We promised to cover it back in the post on meta titles and descriptions — here it is.

What Search Console actually shows you

Think of it as Google's own report card on your site. It tells you:

  • The exact search terms people used before they saw you in the results.
  • Where you ranked for each one, on average.
  • How many people saw you versus how many actually clicked.
  • Any technical problems stopping Google from showing your pages.

That first point is the gold. You'll almost always find people reaching you through searches you'd never have guessed — and that's a map of what to write about, what to put on your homepage, and where the easy wins are.

Setting it up in 15 minutes

You'll need a Google account and access to your website or its settings. The steps:

  1. Go to Google Search Console and sign in with your business Google account — ideally the same one that owns your Google Business Profile, to keep everything in one place.
  2. Add your website as a property. Choose the "Domain" option if you can — it covers every version of your address at once.
  3. Verify you own it. Google gives you a few ways. The simplest for most people is adding a small record through your domain provider, or if your site is on Squarespace, Wix or WordPress, there's usually a built-in field to paste a verification code. Your web person can do this in two minutes if you'd rather not.
  4. Wait a few days. Data doesn't appear instantly — Google needs time to gather it. Come back after a week and there'll be something to look at.
If verification stumps you, don't give up and walk away — it's the one fiddly step, and it's a five-minute job for anyone who manages your site. Getting past it is worth it.

The four things to check each week

Once data's flowing, you don't need to live in there. A five-minute weekly glance at four things is plenty.

You're watching for patterns, not daily wobbles. A term climbing from position 12 to position 8 is a nudge to improve that page — it's almost on page one, and a little work might get it there.

The move that pays for itself

Here's the highest-value habit Search Console unlocks. Sort your queries by impressions, then look for ones where you're getting seen a lot but clicked rarely — high impressions, low clicks, ranking somewhere on page one or two.

Those are pages Google already likes, that people already find, that just aren't compelling enough to click. That's a snippet problem, and it's the cheapest fix in SEO — rewrite the title and description and you can lift clicks without moving your ranking an inch. It's exactly the 15-minute rewrite we covered earlier, except now you know precisely which pages to spend the fifteen minutes on.

Where it fits with everything else

Search Console is the measurement layer under all the local SEO work — it's how you tell whether the profile fixes, the reviews and the on-page changes are actually landing. If you've not done that groundwork yet, the complete local SEO guide is the bigger picture; this is the dashboard that tells you it's working.

Set it up once, glance at it weekly, and you go from hoping Google likes your site to actually knowing. For fifteen minutes and no money, there's very little in marketing that pays back better.

Search Console questions, answered

Completely free, and it always has been. It's Google's own tool for site owners. Anyone with a website should have it set up — it's the closest thing you get to seeing your business through Google's eyes, and it costs nothing but fifteen minutes to connect.

Search Console shows what happens before someone reaches your site — what they searched, where you ranked, whether they clicked. Analytics shows what happens after they arrive — which pages they viewed, how long they stayed, what they did. You want both, but Search Console is the one most small businesses are missing.

A five-minute look once a week is plenty for most small businesses. You're watching for trends, not daily wobbles — clicks trending up or down, new search terms appearing, and any errors flagged under Indexing. Anything more frequent and you'll drive yourself mad reading noise as signal.
