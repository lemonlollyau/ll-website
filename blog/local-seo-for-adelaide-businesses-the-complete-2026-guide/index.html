Local SEO for Adelaide businesses: the complete 2026 guide

By the lemonlolly team·20 July 2026·9 min read

Local SEO has a branding problem. It sounds like something you buy from a bloke who rings you at 4pm on a Thursday, and it gets sold in packages full of words like "authority stacking". Underneath the noise it's actually quite simple: making sure that when someone near you searches for what you do, you're one of the businesses that comes up.

This is the whole picture in one place — what matters, roughly in what order, and honestly what each piece is worth. It's written for an Adelaide business with a normal budget and no in-house marketing person.

How Google decides who ranks locally

Three things drive local results, and it helps enormously to know which is which.

  • Relevance — does what you do match what they searched? This is mostly your categories, your website content and the words you use.
  • Distance — how far are you from the searcher? Largely out of your control.
  • Prominence — how well known and well regarded are you? Reviews, mentions, links, and general reputation.

You can move relevance and prominence. You cannot move distance. This is why "we want to rank number one in Adelaide" isn't a goal you can be held to — there is no single Adelaide result. Someone searching from Semaphore and someone searching from Stirling see different maps, and both are correct.

Local SEO isn't about being first. It's about being visible to the people who are close enough to actually become customers.

Where the effort actually pays

Not all of this is equally worth your time. Roughly:

JobEffortWhat it's worth
Google Business Profile setup and categoriesAn hour or twoVery high — start here
Getting reviews consistentlyOngoing, smallVery high
Homepage and service page copyA dayHigh
Suburb / service-area pagesA day per page, done properlyMedium to high
Citations and directory consistencyHalf a day, onceLow but necessary
Links from other local sitesSlow and ongoingMedium, compounds
Technical bits (speed, schema)VariesLow ceiling, high floor

The pattern: the cheap, unglamorous things at the top matter most. Most businesses skip them and go straight to the fiddly stuff.

1. Get the Google Business Profile right

This is the highest-leverage hour in local marketing, and it's free.

The single most important field is your primary category. It largely determines which searches you're eligible for at all. Choose the term your customers use, not your industry's preferred title — "Electrician", not "Electrical contracting solutions". Add secondary categories for everything else you do.

After that: complete every field, upload real photos of real work, set accurate hours including public holidays, and add your services with actual descriptions. Profiles that are filled in properly simply perform better than ones that aren't.

We've written the field-by-field version separately — work through the 12-point optimisation checklist and you'll have covered it. If you're already set up but can't find yourself on the map, start instead with the diagnosis for why you're not showing up, because there's usually a specific, findable cause.

2. Build a review habit, not a review campaign

Review count, review recency and review content all feed local rankings. They also do the harder job of convincing a stranger to ring you rather than the next result.

The mistake is treating reviews as a project — a big push, forty reviews in a month, then silence for a year. Google notices the silence. A steady trickle beats a burst every time.

The practical version: ask at the moment the customer is happiest, which is usually right after you've finished, not three weeks later in an email. Make it one tap. And never buy them or offer a discount for them — it's against Google's rules, it's obvious to readers, and fake reviews get stripped. The full approach is here, including what to actually say.

Reply to every review, including the bad ones. A calm, non-defensive reply to a one-star does more for a browsing customer than a dozen five-stars.

3. Make your website say where you are and what you do

An astonishing number of Adelaide business websites never plainly state which suburbs they serve. Google can't infer it, and neither can a customer skimming for ten seconds.

Your homepage should make the what and the where obvious above the fold — without reading like it was written for a robot. Every service you offer should have its own page rather than a shared list, because a page about one thing ranks for that thing far better than a page about nine things.

Then there's the two lines Google actually shows in results. Your title and description don't change your ranking much, but they decide whether anyone clicks it. It's a 15-minute job per page and one of the few genuinely quick wins left.

Not sure which phrases to build around? Start from the keywords Adelaide customers actually type rather than the ones that sound impressive in a meeting.

4. Suburb pages — carefully

If you serve a wide area, suburb pages let you show up beyond your immediate postcode. Done properly they work well. Done lazily they're the fastest way to look like spam.

The test is simple: could this page only have been written about this suburb? A page for Norwood that mentions the parking situation on The Parade, the kind of housing stock you work on there, and a job you actually did — that's a real page. The same paragraph with "Norwood" swapped for "Glenelg" forty times is a doorway page, and Google has been filtering those for years.

Three good suburb pages beat thirty thin ones. The detail on doing it without spamming Google is here.

5. Sort your citations once

Citations are mentions of your name, address and phone number on other sites — directories, industry bodies, the local chamber, Yellow Pages, Apple Maps, Bing Places.

They matter for one reason: consistency. If half the web says Unit 4 and the other half says Suite 4, and three listings carry a disconnected mobile number, Google's confidence that these all describe one real business drops.

So: pick the exact format of your details, write it down, and make everything match — character for character. Do the twenty listings that matter, fix the wrong ones, and then stop. Ongoing monthly citation services are, for most small businesses, money you'd rather spend on almost anything else.

6. Earn a few local links

Links from other Adelaide websites are the closest thing to a genuine authority signal, and they're the slowest to get.

The realistic sources for a small business: the sponsorship page of the club you already sponsor, your suppliers' "where to buy" pages, the trade associations you belong to, a local news piece if you do something worth writing about, a genuinely useful guest article. Fringe, Tasting Australia and the local sporting calendar throw up more of these opportunities than people expect.

What doesn't work: buying links, mass guest posting, anything offering "100 backlinks". At best wasted money, at worst an actual penalty.

7. Don't ignore the boring technical layer

Technical SEO rarely wins you rankings. It just stops you losing them.

Make sure the site works properly on a phone, since most local searches happen on one. Make sure it loads quickly — slow pages cost you customers before Google even gets involved. Add LocalBusiness schema so your details are machine-readable. Make sure Google Search Console is set up so you can see what's actually happening.

None of this is exciting. All of it is the floor you build on.

What to measure, and what to ignore

The vanity metric is "where do I rank" — which, as established, has no single answer. Better questions:

  1. Calls, direction requests and website clicks from your profile. Google reports these. They're closer to money than any ranking number.
  2. Search Console clicks and impressions for your money pages, month on month.
  3. Actual enquiries. Ask people how they found you. It's unfashionable, low-tech, and more accurate than most dashboards.

And remember that ranking is only half the job — traffic that lands on a page which doesn't convert is just expensive attention. Worth checking whether your site actually turns visits into enquiries before you spend more getting people there.

The honest summary

If you only ever do three things: fix your Google Business Profile categories, build a steady review habit, and write plain service pages that say what you do and where you do it. That's most of the available benefit for a small Adelaide business, and none of it requires an agency.

The rest — suburb pages, citations, links, schema — is worth doing, but it's refinement. Doing the refinement while the basics are broken is the single most common way local marketing money gets wasted.

Local SEO questions, answered

Profile fixes can shift things in days. Reviews and on-page work usually show up over one to three months. Anything involving links or genuine authority is a six-month-plus job. Adelaide is a smaller market than Sydney or Melbourne, so competition is often thinner and results can come faster — but anyone promising page one in a fortnight is guessing.

Only for suburbs where you genuinely work and can write something specific about working there. A handful of real, substantial suburb pages beat forty near-identical ones with the name swapped out — Google filters those as doorway pages, and they can drag down the rest of your site.

They're worth getting right once, then leaving alone. Consistent name, address and phone details across the major directories help Google trust your business is real. But citations are a hygiene factor, not a growth lever — paying a monthly fee to submit you to hundreds of directories is close to worthless.

Your Google Business Profile, and specifically the primary category. It decides which searches you're even eligible to appear for, it takes ten minutes to change, and it's free. No amount of website work compensates for having the wrong category set.
