Why your Adelaide business isn't showing up on Google Maps

By the lemonlolly team·20 July 2026·5 min read

It's a genuinely horrible feeling. You type your own trade and suburb into Google — "electrician Prospect", say — and there on the map are three businesses you've never heard of, one of them apparently operating out of a unit in Salisbury. You're nowhere.

Before you assume Google has it in for you: it almost certainly hasn't. Maps invisibility usually comes down to a handful of unglamorous causes, and most of them have a fix you can do yourself this week.

First, make sure you're actually invisible

Start here, because half the businesses that ring us about this aren't missing at all.

When you search for your business, Google already knows who you are. You're signed into the account that owns the profile, you've clicked your own listing a hundred times, and you're very likely sitting inside the building. Of course you can see it.

The honest test: open a browser in private mode on a different device, switch off wi-fi so you're on mobile data, and search the way a customer would — the service and the suburb, not your business name. Better still, text a friend in a different part of Adelaide and ask what they get. If they can see you, you're not invisible. You're just not ranking. Different problem, and a more solvable one.

Proximity is the lever you can't pull

This is the part nobody enjoys hearing. Distance between the searcher and your business is one of the heaviest factors in Maps rankings, and you can't optimise your way around geography.

Someone standing on Rundle Mall searching "coffee" gets Rundle Mall cafés. Your excellent café in Glenelg does not appear, and no amount of profile tinkering will change that. The map results reshuffle every time the searcher moves.

So the useful question isn't "why aren't I number one for my industry in Adelaide" — nobody is, because there's no such thing. It's "am I showing up for people who are actually near me, or near the areas I serve?" That's a question you can do something about.

Stop searching from your desk and calling it research. Your rankings look completely different from a phone in Norwood than they do from the back office in Kent Town.

The five things that usually keep you off the map

Once you've ruled out "it's just proximity", these are the culprits we see most often:

  • You're not verified, or your verification lapsed. An unverified profile can sit there looking perfectly complete and still not compete. Google also re-triggers verification after big edits — change your address or business name and you can quietly drop off until you confirm it again.
  • Your primary category is wrong. This is the big one. Google leans hard on your primary category to decide which searches you're eligible for. A business listed as "Building consultant" will not surface for "builder". Pick the category that matches what customers type, not the one that sounds most impressive.
  • There's a duplicate listing. Old profiles from a previous owner, a former address, or an auto-generated listing Google created itself will split your reviews and confuse the signals. Search your business name plus a couple of variations and see what turns up.
  • Your details disagree with each other. If your website says Unit 4 and your profile says Suite 4, and your Facebook page still has the 2019 phone number, Google gets less confident it's all the same business. Confidence matters.
  • You've been suspended and haven't noticed. It happens more than people realise, usually after an address change or a category that looked like keyword stuffing. Sign in and look for a notice at the top of the dashboard — a suspended profile disappears from Maps entirely.

Working through these properly is a job of an hour or two. We've written the field-by-field version as a 12-point optimisation checklist — this post is the diagnosis, that one is the treatment.

If you don't have a shopfront

Plumbers, sparkies, mobile groomers, cleaners — anyone who goes to the customer rather than the other way round — need to set the profile up as a service-area business. That means hiding your street address and listing the suburbs or regions you cover instead.

Two things people get wrong here. First, they list half of South Australia. A service area covering everything from Gawler to Victor Harbor tells Google nothing useful; keep it to where you'd genuinely drive on a Tuesday. Second, they leave a home address showing, which is both a privacy issue and, if it's a residential address you can't verify properly, a suspension risk.

Service-area businesses also can't rely on the map alone. Your website has to do the local lifting, which is where properly built suburb pages earn their keep.

What to do this week

  1. Test properly. Private browsing, different device, mobile data, service-plus-suburb search. Write down what you actually see.
  2. Check for a suspension or pending verification. Sign in to your profile and read the notices. Takes two minutes and rules out the worst case.
  3. Fix your primary category. Change it to the words your customers use. Add secondary categories for the rest of what you do.
  4. Hunt for duplicates. Search your name, your old name, your old address. Report anything that shouldn't be there.
  5. Make your name, address and phone number identical everywhere. Website, profile, socials, directories. Character for character.
  6. Get some reviews moving. Review count and recency feed local rankings, and they're the one thing your competitors can't copy — without being a pest about it.

One honest caveat to finish on: none of this is instant. Category and detail changes can take days to a few weeks to settle, and reviews build slowly by design. If someone promises to have you at the top of the map pack by Friday, keep your wallet shut.

Maps questions, answered

Because Google knows it's you. You're signed into the account that owns the profile, you've searched for it before, and you're probably standing in the shop. Your customers get results based on where they are, not where you are. Check from a different device on mobile data, or ask a mate in another suburb what they see.

A new verified profile usually appears within a few days, but ranking well takes longer — often a couple of months of reviews, photos and consistent details before you hold a spot in the local pack. If nothing has appeared after two weeks, check your profile isn't suspended or pending a second verification.

Yes, and it's the single biggest factor you can't do much about. Google ranks Maps results heavily on proximity to the searcher, so a business in the CBD will beat you in the CBD. That's why service-area businesses should target the suburbs they actually serve rather than fighting for a postcode they aren't in.
